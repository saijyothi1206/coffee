Preparation Techniques

Coffee preparation involves roasting raw beans, grinding them to the appropriate size, and brewing by mixing grounds with hot or cold water based on the method used. Popular brewing methods include French press, pour-over, and drip, each requiring specific grind sizes, water temperature (about 90-96°C), coffee-to-water ratios, and timing to extract optimal flavor. The brewed liquid is then separated from the grounds and can be customized with milk, sugar, or flavorings before serving.

Bean Selection

Coffee bean selection primarily depends on choosing between Arabica and Robusta beans. Arabica, grown at higher altitudes, offers smooth, slightly acidic flavors and is considered superior quality. Robusta, grown lower, is stronger and more bitter, often used in instant coffee. Specialty-grade, freshly roasted, ethically sourced Arabica beans deliver the best flavor and health benefits. Consider origin, processing, and roast level for tailored taste preferences.

Freshness of coffee

Coffee freshness hinges on green bean quality, proper storage, and post-roast handling. Fresh green beans show vibrant, even color with slight sheen and retain moisture, crucial for flavor retention. After roasting, coffee requires 12–24 hours of degassing to reach optimal flavor. Fresh roasted beans emit strong aroma and release CO₂, creating a visible bloom during brewing. Stale beans appear dull, lose aroma, and taste flat or woody. Packaging with good barrier properties prolongs freshness by slowing oxidation and moisture loss.
